1. What Is Linear Regression? — Intuition First
Imagine you are a real-estate analyst. You have a spreadsheet with two columns: the square footage of every house sold in your county last year, and the price each house fetched. You scatter-plot these points and notice something obvious — bigger houses tend to cost more. The points do not form a perfect line, but there is a clear upward trend. Your job is to draw the single straight line that best captures that trend, so that when a new house hits the market you can plug in its square footage and get a sensible price estimate.
That line is your linear regression model, and it is described by one of the most important equations in all of statistics:
ŷ = β₀ + β₁x
Let us unpack every symbol:
ŷ (y-hat) — the predicted value of the target variable (e.g., predicted house price). The hat distinguishes it from y, the actual observed value.
β₀ (beta-zero) — the intercept, sometimes called the bias term. It is the predicted value of y when x = 0. Geometrically, it is where the line crosses the vertical axis.
β₁ (beta-one) — the slope, also called the weight or coefficient. It tells you how much ŷ changes for a one-unit increase in x. If β₁ = 200, then each additional square foot adds $200 to the predicted price.
x — the feature, predictor, or independent variable (e.g., square footage).

Simple Linear Regression: Interpreting the model ŷ = β₀ + β₁x through the intercept, slope, predicted value, and observed data points.
Geometric Interpretation
In feature space, each data point (xᵢ, yᵢ) is a dot on a two-dimensional plane. The regression line sweeps through this cloud of dots, positioned so that the overall vertical distance between the dots and the line is as small as possible. In higher dimensions (multiple regression), this “line” generalises to a hyperplane, but the principle is identical.
Beginner’s Corner
Think of the regression line as a taut rubber band laid across your scatter plot. Gravity pulls the band towards every data point. The position where the band naturally settles — balancing all those pulls — is approximately where the best-fit line lives.
The goal is deceptively simple: find the values of β₀ and β₁ that minimise the total distance between our predicted values (ŷ) and the actual observed values (y). But “distance” needs a precise definition — and that brings us to the cost function.
2. The Cost Function — Mean Squared Error (MSE)
For each data point i, the residual (or error) is the gap between the actual value and our prediction:
eᵢ = yᵢ − ŷᵢ = yᵢ − (β₀ + β₁xᵢ)
We want all residuals to be small. But we cannot simply add them up — positive and negative residuals would cancel out, giving a misleadingly small total. Two natural fixes exist:
Take absolute values: Σ|eᵢ|. This works intuitively, but the absolute-value function has a sharp corner at zero and is not differentiable everywhere, making calculus-based optimisation harder.
Square the residuals: Σeᵢ². Squaring is smooth, differentiable everywhere, and has a bonus: it penalises large errors disproportionately. A residual of 10 contributes 100 to the sum, but a residual of 2 contributes only 4. This is usually desirable because large prediction errors are typically much worse than small ones.
We choose squaring and average over all n data points to get the Mean Squared Error:
J(β₀, β₁) = (1/n) Σᵢ₌₁ⁿ (yᵢ − β₀ − β₁xᵢ)²
This function J is our cost function (also called the loss function or objective function). Our entire task reduces to: find β₀ and β₁ that minimise J.
The Cost Surface
Visualise J as a surface hovering over the (β₀, β₁) plane. For every pair of parameter values, J gives a height — the average squared error. Because J is a sum of squared terms (each quadratic in β₀ and β₁), the surface is a convex paraboloid: a smooth, bowl-shaped surface with a single lowest point.

Pro Tip
Convexity is a gift. It guarantees that any local minimum is also the global minimum. This means gradient descent (Section 4) will always converge to the optimal solution for linear regression, regardless of where we start. Many advanced models (neural networks, for instance) lose this guarantee — one reason linear regression remains a gold-standard baseline.
3. Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) — The Closed-Form Solution
Because J is a smooth, convex function of β₀ and β₁, we can find its minimum analytically by setting the partial derivatives to zero. This classical approach is called Ordinary Least Squares.
3.1 Scalar Derivation (Two Parameters)
Step 1 — Write out the cost function:
J(β₀, β₁) = (1/n) Σᵢ₌₁ⁿ (yᵢ − β₀ − β₁xᵢ)²
Step 2 — Partial derivative with respect to β₀:
∂J/∂β₀ = (1/n) Σᵢ₌₁ⁿ 2(yᵢ − β₀ − β₁xᵢ)(−1) = −(2/n) Σᵢ₌₁ⁿ (yᵢ − β₀ − β₁xᵢ)
Set this equal to zero:
Σ (yᵢ − β₀ − β₁xᵢ) = 0
Σ yᵢ − nβ₀ − β₁ Σ xᵢ = 0
nȳ − nβ₀ − β₁ · nx̄ = 0
β₀ = ȳ − β₁x̄ … (Normal Equation 1)
Step 3 — Partial derivative with respect to β₁:
∂J/∂β₁ = (1/n) Σᵢ₌₁ⁿ 2(yᵢ − β₀ − β₁xᵢ)(−xᵢ) = −(2/n) Σᵢ₌₁ⁿ xᵢ(yᵢ − β₀ − β₁xᵢ)
Set to zero:
Σ xᵢ(yᵢ − β₀ − β₁xᵢ) = 0
Σ xᵢyᵢ − β₀ Σ xᵢ − β₁ Σ xᵢ² = 0
Step 4 — Substitute Normal Equation 1 (β₀ = ȳ − β₁x̄) into the equation above:
Σ xᵢyᵢ − (ȳ − β₁x̄) Σ xᵢ − β₁ Σ xᵢ² = 0
Σ xᵢyᵢ − ȳ Σ xᵢ + β₁x̄ Σ xᵢ − β₁ Σ xᵢ² = 0
Σ xᵢyᵢ − nȳx̄ = β₁ (Σ xᵢ² − nx̄²)
Σ (xᵢ − x̄)(yᵢ − ȳ) = β₁ · Σ (xᵢ − x̄)²
β₁ = Σ (xᵢ − x̄)(yᵢ − ȳ) / Σ (xᵢ − x̄)² = Cov(X, Y) / Var(X)
And from Normal Equation 1:
β₀ = ȳ − β₁x̄
Beginner’s Corner
What does β₁ = Cov(X,Y)/Var(X) actually mean?
The covariance measures how X and Y move together. The variance of X measures how spread out X is. Dividing one by the other answers: “For each unit of spread in X, how much does Y move with it?” That is exactly the slope.
3.2 Matrix Formulation
For computational elegance and generalisation to multiple regression, we rewrite everything in matrix form. Stack all observations into vectors and matrices:
Let y = [y₁, y₂, …, yₙ]ᵀ (n × 1 vector of targets)
Let X = a matrix where row i is [1, xᵢ] (n × 2 design matrix, the column of 1s handles the intercept)
Let β = [β₀, β₁]ᵀ (2 × 1 parameter vector)
The model becomes ŷ = Xβ, and the cost function in matrix form is:
J(β) = (1/n)(y − Xβ)ᵀ(y − Xβ)
Expand the product:
J(β) = (1/n)(yᵀy − yᵀXβ − βᵀXᵀy + βᵀXᵀXβ)
Since yᵀXβ is a scalar, it equals its transpose: βᵀXᵀy
J(β) = (1/n)(yᵀy − 2βᵀXᵀy + βᵀXᵀXβ)
Take the gradient with respect to β (using matrix calculus identities: ∇β(βᵀa) = a and ∇β(βᵀAβ) = 2Aβ for symmetric A):
∇β J = (1/n)(−2Xᵀy + 2XᵀXβ)
Set the gradient to zero:
XᵀXβ = Xᵀy
These are the famous Normal Equations. Solving for β:
β = (XᵀX)⁻¹Xᵀy
Important: Invertibility Condition
The matrix XᵀX must be invertible (i.e., full rank) for this solution to exist. This fails when columns of X are linearly dependent — a symptom of perfect multicollinearity. In practice, near-multicollinearity (high correlation between features) makes XᵀX nearly singular, causing numerically unstable estimates with huge variances. Regularisation (Section 8) is the remedy.
3.3 Computational Complexity
Computing (XᵀX)⁻¹Xᵀy requires O(n·p²) for the matrix multiplication XᵀX (where p is the number of features) and O(p³) for the inversion. For simple linear regression (p = 2), this is trivial. But when p is in the tens of thousands (e.g., text data with bag-of-words features), inverting a p × p matrix becomes prohibitively expensive — motivating iterative methods like gradient descent.
4. Gradient Descent — The Iterative Approach
When the dataset is massive or the feature space is high-dimensional, the O(p³) cost of matrix inversion becomes a bottleneck.
Gradient descent offers an iterative alternative: instead of solving for the minimum analytically, we walk downhill on the cost surface, step by step, until we reach the bottom of the bowl.
4.1 The Algorithm
Initialise: Set β₀ and β₁ to some starting values (zeros, random values, or a heuristic).
Repeat until convergence:
Compute the gradients:
∂J/∂β₀ = −(2/n) Σᵢ₌₁ⁿ (yᵢ − β₀ − β₁xᵢ)
∂J/∂β₁ = −(2/n) Σᵢ₌₁ⁿ xᵢ · (yᵢ − β₀ − β₁xᵢ)
Update the parameters simultaneously:
β₀ := β₀ − α · ∂J/∂β₀
β₁ := β₁ − α · ∂J/∂β₁
Here, α (alpha) is the learning rate — a small positive number that controls the step size.
Beginner’s Corner
Picture yourself blindfolded on a hilly landscape. You can feel the slope of the ground under your feet. Gradient descent says: “Take a step in the direction where the ground slopes downward most steeply.” Repeat. Eventually you reach the valley floor. The learning rate is your step size — too big and you overshoot the valley; too small and you take forever to get there.
4.2 The Learning Rate — Getting It Right

4.3 Convergence Criteria
Common stopping rules include:
(a) the change in J between iterations falls below a threshold ε (e.g., 10⁻⁶);
(b) the magnitude of the gradient falls below ε; or
(c) a maximum number of iterations is reached.

4.5 Feature Scaling
When features are on wildly different scales (e.g., square footage in thousands vs. number of bedrooms in single digits), the cost surface becomes a narrow, elongated ellipse rather than a nice circle. Gradient descent zigzags inefficiently down the narrow valley.
Feature scaling fixes this:
Standardisation (Z-score): x’ = (x − μ) / σ. Centers data at mean 0, scales to unit variance.
Min-Max Normalisation: x’ = (x − x_min) / (x_max − x_min). Rescales to [0, 1].
Pro Tip
Feature scaling is critical for gradient descent but irrelevant for OLS. The closed-form solution handles unscaled features perfectly because it solves the system directly. If you are using gradient descent, always scale first.

5. Assumptions of Linear Regression — A Deep Dive
Linear regression is not magic — it comes with a contract. If the assumptions below hold, OLS estimates are the Best Linear Unbiased Estimators (BLUE), as guaranteed by the Gauss-Markov theorem. When they are violated, the estimates may be biased, inefficient, or misleading.
5.1 Linearity
What it means: The true relationship between X and Y is linear (or at least well-approximated by a line).
Why it matters: If the real relationship is a curve, a straight line will systematically miss the pattern, producing biased predictions.
How to test: Plot residuals vs. fitted values. If you see a curve or funnel shape, linearity is violated. The Rainbow test provides a formal statistical check.
If violated: Add polynomial features (x², x³), apply log or square-root transformations, or switch to a nonlinear model.
5.2 Independence of Errors
What it means: The residual for observation i tells you nothing about the residual for observation j. Each error is its own draw.
Why it matters: Correlated errors (common in time-series data) cause standard errors to be underestimated, leading to overconfident hypothesis tests.
How to test: The Durbin-Watson test detects first-order autocorrelation. Values near 2 indicate independence; near 0 or 4 indicate positive or negative autocorrelation.
If violated: Use time-series models (ARIMA), add lagged variables, or use Newey-West standard errors.
5.3 Homoscedasticity
What it means: The variance of the residuals is constant across all levels of X (the errors do not “fan out”).
Why it matters: Heteroscedasticity does not bias β estimates, but it does invalidate standard errors, confidence intervals, and hypothesis tests.
How to test: Residuals vs. fitted plot (look for a funnel shape); Breusch-Pagan test; Goldfeld-Quandt test.
If violated: Weighted Least Squares (WLS), robust standard errors (HC0–HC3), or log-transforming the target variable.
5.4 Normality of Residuals
What it means: The errors ε are drawn from a normal (Gaussian) distribution.
Why it matters: Normality is required for valid confidence intervals and p-values in small samples. With large samples, the Central Limit Theorem makes inference approximately valid even without normality.
How to test: Q-Q plot (points should follow a 45° line); Shapiro-Wilk test; histogram of residuals.
Write on Medium
If violated: Transform the target (Box-Cox), use bootstrapped confidence intervals, or increase sample size.
5.5 No Multicollinearity
What it means: No predictor is a near-perfect linear combination of other predictors. (Strictly relevant in multiple regression, but the concept is introduced here.)
Why it matters: High multicollinearity inflates the variance of coefficient estimates, making them unstable and hard to interpret.
How to test: Compute the Variance Inflation Factor (VIF) for each predictor. VIF > 5–10 signals a problem. Also inspect the correlation matrix.
If violated: Remove correlated features, combine them via PCA, or use Ridge regression (L2 regularisation).
5.6 No Endogeneity
What it means: E[ε | X] = 0 — the errors are uncorrelated with the predictors. Formally, there are no omitted variables that are correlated with both X and Y.
Why it matters: Endogeneity biases the OLS estimates, meaning you cannot trust the coefficients for causal interpretation.
If violated: Instrumental Variables (IV) estimation, Two-Stage Least Squares (2SLS), or adding the omitted variable if possible.
Common Pitfall
Many beginners check normality and homoscedasticity but forget about endogeneity. If your goal is causal inference (not just prediction), endogeneity is the most dangerous assumption to violate — and the hardest to test for.

6. Evaluation Metrics — Measuring Model Performance
A model is only as good as our ability to measure how well it performs. Below are the essential regression metrics, each with its formula, interpretation, and trade-offs.
6.1 R² — Coefficient of Determination
R² = 1 − (SSres / SStot)
where SSres = Σ(yᵢ − ŷᵢ)² and SStot = Σ(yᵢ − ȳ)²
Interpretation: R² tells you the proportion of variance in Y explained by the model.
An R² of 0.85 means 85% of the variability in the target is captured by the line; the remaining 15% is unexplained noise. Range: 0 to 1 for a reasonable model (can technically be negative if the model is worse than predicting the mean).
Key insight for simple linear regression: R² equals the square of the Pearson correlation coefficient r. If r = 0.9, then R² = 0.81.
6.2 Adjusted R²
Adjusted R² = 1 − [(1 − R²)(n − 1) / (n − p − 1)]
R² never decreases when you add a new feature — even a useless one.
Adjusted R² penalises model complexity by accounting for the number of predictors p.
If a new feature does not improve the model enough to justify the lost degree of freedom, Adjusted R² will decrease even as R² ticks up. Always use Adjusted R² when comparing models with different numbers of features.
6.3 MSE, RMSE, MAE, and MAPE

Pro Tip
Use RMSE as your default metric for reporting. Use MAE if your dataset has significant outliers that you do not want to dominate the evaluation. Use MAPE only when you need scale-free comparison and your target values are well away from zero.
7. The Bias-Variance Tradeoff
This is arguably the single most important concept in all of statistical learning. It explains why models fail and how to choose the right level of complexity.
7.1 Definitions
Bias: Error from oversimplifying the model. A model with high bias “misses the point” — it cannot capture the true pattern even with infinite training data. This is underfitting.
Variance: Error from the model being too sensitive to the specific training data. A model with high variance fits the training set perfectly but performs poorly on new data because it has memorised noise. This is overfitting.
Irreducible error (σ²): The noise inherent in the data itself. No model can eliminate it.
7.2 The Decomposition
For a new test point x₀, the expected prediction error can be decomposed as:
E[(y − ŷ)²] = Bias(ŷ)² + Var(ŷ) + σ²
Derivation sketch:
Let y = f(x₀) + ε where E[ε] = 0 and Var(ε) = σ²
E[(y − ŷ)²] = E[(f(x₀) + ε − ŷ)²]
= E[(f(x₀) − ŷ)² + 2ε(f(x₀) − ŷ) + ε²]
= E[(f(x₀) − ŷ)²] + 0 + σ² (since ε is independent of ŷ and E[ε]=0)
Now decompose E[(f(x₀) − ŷ)²] by adding and subtracting E[ŷ]:
= E[(f(x₀) − E[ŷ] + E[ŷ] − ŷ)²]
= (f(x₀) − E[ŷ])² + E[(ŷ − E[ŷ])²] + 2(f(x₀) − E[ŷ])E[ŷ − E[ŷ]]
= (f(x₀) − E[ŷ])² + Var(ŷ) + 0
= Bias(ŷ)² + Var(ŷ) + σ²
7.3 Application to Linear Regression

Simple linear regression (ŷ = β₀ + β₁x): If the true relationship is nonlinear, this model has high bias (it cannot capture the curve) but low variance (a line does not wiggle much between different training sets). This is underfitting.
High-degree polynomial regression (ŷ = β₀ + β₁x + β₂x² + … + β₁₀x¹⁰): Very flexible — low bias — but it will fit the noise in the training data, leading to high variance. This is overfitting.
The sweet spot: A model complex enough to capture the true signal but simple enough to ignore the noise. Finding this balance is the central challenge of machine learning.
Important
The bias-variance tradeoff is why we always evaluate on a held-out test set (or via cross-validation), never on the training set alone. A training-set metric tells you about bias. A test-set metric reveals the combined effect of bias and variance.
8. Regularisation Preview
Regularisation is a technique to combat overfitting by penalising large coefficient values. It adds a term to the cost function that discourages the model from becoming too complex.
Ridge Regression (L2 Regularisation)
JRidge(β) = (1/n) Σ(yᵢ − ŷᵢ)² + λ Σ βⱼ²
The penalty λΣβⱼ² shrinks coefficients towards zero but never exactly to zero. This reduces variance at the cost of a small increase in bias. The hyperparameter λ controls the strength of the penalty.
Lasso Regression (L1 Regularisation)
JLasso(β) = (1/n) Σ(yᵢ − ŷᵢ)² + λ Σ |βⱼ|
The L1 penalty can shrink some coefficients exactly to zero, effectively performing feature selection. This is invaluable when you suspect many features are irrelevant.
Looking Ahead
We will dive deeply into Ridge, Lasso, and Elastic Net regularisation in a future week of this series. For now, remember the core idea: adding a complexity penalty to the cost function trades a small increase in bias for a large reduction in variance, often improving generalisation.
9. Case Study: Predicting California Housing Prices

β₁ ≈ 0.42: For every one-unit increase in median income (approximately $10,000), the predicted median house value increases by about $42,000. This is a powerful, interpretable insight.
β₀ ≈ 0.45: A block group with zero median income would have a predicted house value of ~$45,000. (This is an extrapolation beyond the data range and should not be taken literally.)

9.8 Key Findings
Median income is the single strongest predictor of house values, explaining ~47% of variance on its own.
The model is simple, interpretable, and does not overfit — but it underfits because housing prices depend on many factors (location, house age, supply, etc.).
Adding more features (multiple regression) and capturing nonlinear effects (polynomial terms) would significantly improve performance — exactly what we will tackle in Week 2.
Pro Tip
A simple linear regression model is the perfect baseline. Before training any complex model (random forest, neural network, etc.), always fit a linear regression first. If your fancy model cannot beat this baseline, something is wrong with your pipeline — not your model choice.
10. Key Takeaways
Linear regression is foundational — not trivial. The mathematics of OLS, gradient descent, and the bias-variance tradeoff underpin nearly every advanced model in machine learning.
The OLS closed-form solution β = (XᵀX)⁻¹Xᵀy is elegant but limited to cases where XᵀX is invertible and p is not too large.
Gradient descent is the general-purpose optimiser that scales to any dataset size and any differentiable cost function — the engine of deep learning.
The MSE cost function is convex for linear regression, guaranteeing a unique global minimum. This is a luxury not available to most nonlinear models.
Always check assumptions — linearity, independence, homoscedasticity, normality, no multicollinearity, no endogeneity. Violations do not necessarily mean the model is useless, but they change what you can infer from it.
R² is not enough. A high R² does not mean the model is good; a low R² does not mean it is useless. Always examine residual plots and use multiple metrics.
The bias-variance tradeoff is the central tension in modelling: simplicity (bias) vs. flexibility (variance). The goal is always generalisation, not memorisation.
Common Beginner Mistakes
Not plotting the data first. Always visualise before modelling. A scatter plot takes seconds and can save hours of debugging.
Using R² on the training set alone. This tells you nothing about how the model will perform on new data.
Forgetting to check residual plots. Coefficient values and R² can look fine even when assumptions are badly violated.
Applying gradient descent without feature scaling. This leads to painfully slow convergence or divergence.
Confusing correlation with causation. A strong β₁ means X and Y move together — not that X causes Y.
Extrapolating beyond the data range. The linear relationship is only validated within the observed range of X. Beyond that, all bets are off.
When to Use and When NOT to Use : Linear Regression
Use Linear Regression When…
The relationship is approximately linear — the signal follows a straight‑line trend and residuals don’t show systematic curvature.
Interpretability is paramount — regulatory, medical, policy, or audit‑heavy contexts where coefficients must be explainable.
You need a fast, reliable baseline — OLS gives a quick benchmark for more complex models.
Assumptions are reasonably met — or you understand the violations and have mitigation strategies (e.g., transformations, robust SEs).
Data dimensionality is moderate — feature count is manageable and multicollinearity is low or controlled.
Avoid Linear Regression When…
The relationship is clearly nonlinear — and transformations or feature engineering cannot linearize it.
Prediction accuracy is the sole goal — and you have enough data to justify more flexible models (trees, ensembles, neural nets).
The target is categorical — use logistic regression or other classifiers instead.
Strong multicollinearity exists — and you are not using any regularization strategy (Ridge, Lasso, Elastic Net).
Assumptions are severely violated — e.g., heteroscedasticity, autocorrelation, non-normal residuals, or endogeneity.

Historical Foundations:
Galton, F. (1886). “Regression Towards Mediocrity in Hereditary Stature.” Journal of the Anthropological Institute of Great Britain and Ireland, 15, 246–263. — The paper that gave “regression” its name, documenting how children’s heights regress toward the population mean.
Pearson, K. (1896). “Mathematical Contributions to the Theory of Evolution — III. Legendre, A. M. (1805). Nouvelles méthodes pour la détermination des orbites des comètes. — Contains the first published account of the method of least squares, developed independently of Gauss.
Gauss, C. F. (1809). Theoria Motus Corporum Coelestium. — Gauss claimed to have used least squares as early as 1795; this work provides his full derivation.
